At a glance

Who the Community Coordinator is

The Community Coordinator is university staff responsible for groups and social spaces on Intoto: reviewing community creation requests, publishing official communities, monitoring active groups, and keeping conversations appropriate. They are the operational owner of community quality and safety within one university — the role that approves the student-created communities that would otherwise stay hidden.

The one-paragraph version

A coordinator starts the day clearing the pending community queue — approving legitimate groups and rejecting policy violations with a clear reason — then creates official communities that go live instantly, monitors active groups, and moderates content and reports to keep the platform safe.

1

2

0

4

A day in the role

How the work flows

The coordinator's loop centres on the pending queue: review, decide, publish, and keep live groups healthy.

1

Sign in & check the queue

Land on a community-centric dashboard and read the Pending Communities count first — student requests shouldn't be left waiting.

2

Review & decide

Open each request, check the name and description against policy, then approve to publish or reject with a clear, actionable reason.

3

Launch official groups

Create university-run communities that go live instantly, and post welcome content or announcements inside them.

4

Monitor & moderate

Scan active communities and feeds, remove violating content where tools exist, and work reports through to resolution.

5

Communicate

Chat with creators — especially after a rejection — to explain policy and guide a successful resubmission.

Community feature

Overview — topic-based discussions

The Community feature lets people at a university create and join topic-based groups, post updates, and interact in a shared feed. Coordinators (and University Super Admins on web) can create communities and govern requests; students and Third-Party Coordinators can propose new communities, join approved ones, and take part in discussions. Requires the paid Community module to be Active for the tenant.

What Community unlocks

Create or request topic communities → approve requests into a live directory → members join, post, like, comment, and share → coordinators moderate feeds and reports. Feeds always surface the latest post. Chat and profile views stay available alongside community activity.

Roles & permissions

A university can staff any number of Community Coordinators as a team. Approvals and official creation also sit with the University Super Admin on the web portal.

1. Community Coordinator

Operational owner of groups and safety for one university (also known informally as social-media coordinator).

Can do
Create communities on various topics — published instantly (no student approval wait)
Review and approve or reject community creation requests from students or other roles
Moderate discussions and remove inappropriate content
Delete or archive communities when needed
See community feeds with the latest post surfaced
Like, comment, and share posts
Initiate chat with users and view profiles
Handle pending creation requests and community reports
Work as part of a team — any number of Community Coordinators can share the queue

2. Student

Can do
Submit a community creation request — goes for approval (not live until approved)
Join approved communities and participate in discussions
Post content in communities they have joined
View posts in joined communities; feeds show the latest post
Like, comment, and share posts

3. Third-Party Coordinator

Can do

Join approved communities and participate in discussions
Receive suggestions for joining communities relevant to their interests or role
Post content in communities they have joined
See feeds with the latest post; like, comment, and share
Initiate chat with users and view profiles

University Super Admin (web): Can create communities instantly and clear the pending request queue (approve/reject), plus moderate from the web Community module — same governance outcomes as the Community Coordinator, for university-wide oversight.

Community creation workflow

Coordinator / Super Admin creation

  1. Community Coordinator (or University Super Admin on web) creates a community on a topic.
  2. The community publishes instantly — no pending approval step.
  3. It becomes visible to relevant roles (Students, Third-Party Coordinators, and staff who can browse communities).

Student or Third-Party Coordinator request

  1. Student or Third-Party Coordinator submits a request to create a community.
  2. The request lands in the pending queue for Community Coordinator / University Super Admin.
  3. Approved — community is created and visible to relevant users; requester is notified.
  4. Rejected — community does not go live; requester receives a notification with the rejection reason.

01

Getting in

Sign-in is via university credentials. Multi-role accounts choose Community Coordinator, and the dashboard loads with community-centric Quick Way cards and sections — not student travel alerts or event approval queues.

  1. Open Intoto and sign in with email/password or SSO.
  2. Wait for the profile and role payload to finish loading.
  3. Single role → land on the Community Coordinator dashboard; multi-role → choose the role at Role Selection.
  4. A new role invite shows an alert — Accept now or Do it later (deferred invites wait under the envelope).
  5. The four tabs appear — Home, Community, Chats, Profile.
Product

The role's dashboard is configured around groups, so the coordinator's first screen is already their workspace — not a generic menu.

QA focus

Single-role lands on the Community Coordinator dashboard; multi-role shows Role Selection; switching roles reloads community-centric sections (no event-approval or student-travel content).

Marketing

"Open the app to a workspace built for community — your pending queue is right there, ready."

02

The dashboard

Home is an API-driven overview centred on groups and members: a branded header, Quick Way counts (total communities, members, pending queue, active), quick actions, and pending/active community carousels — sometimes with a community analytics chart.

  1. Land on Home; confirm the university logo and name match the institution you support.
  2. Read Quick Way — start with Pending Communities so student requests aren't delayed.
  3. Tap a card to open the list behind it (all communities, members, pending, or active).
  4. Swipe the Pending and Active carousels; use View All for the full lists.
  5. Pull to refresh after bulk approvals so counts stay accurate.
Product

The dashboard deliberately omits student rosters, invites, and event queues — it shows only what's needed to keep communities healthy, which keeps moderation focused.

QA focus

Community-centric cards render (not Total Students, Unassigned, or event queues unless dual-purpose); cards route correctly; carousels and View All work; pull-to-refresh updates counts after approvals.

Marketing

"See every group at a glance — what's pending, what's live, and how your communities are growing."

03

Approving communities

This is the heart of the role and the main safety gate before a group reaches student discovery. Each pending request carries everything needed to make a governance decision — name, description, images, category, visibility, and submitter.

  1. Open a request from Pending Communities on Home or the pending list.
  2. Review the name and description for policy compliance (impersonation, hate speech, duplicates of official groups).
  3. Confirm images, category, and visibility settings are appropriate.
  4. Approve → the community goes live and leaves the queue; the creator is notified.
  5. Reject → enter a clear, actionable reason (empty reasons are blocked); the creator can fix and resubmit.
Product

Requiring a reason on rejection turns a "no" into guidance — the creator learns exactly what to change, which improves the next submission instead of just blocking it.

QA focus

Approve publishes and removes from the queue and updates counts; reject requires a non-empty reason; rejected requests leave the public queue and can be resubmitted; the creator receives the right notification.

Marketing

"Every student group gets a careful, human review — so the communities students join are real and safe."

04

Creating official communities

Coordinators create university-run communities — program houses, international student associations, and the like — that publish immediately, without the approval wait students face. The university vouches for these groups, and the coordinator is a trusted publisher.

  1. Open Create Community from the Community tab or the Home quick action.
  2. Enter name and description (required).
  3. Upload profile and cover images if required.
  4. Set category, campus, rules, and visibility, then tap Submit.
  5. See "Community created successfully!" — it's active and discoverable to students immediately.
Product

The instant-publish path for staff (vs. the student approval flow) encodes trust in the data model — same form, different outcome based on who's creating.

QA focus

Required fields block submit; success message shows; the community is live immediately (no self-approval step); a duplicate name surfaces an inline or server error.

Marketing

"Stand up official communities the moment the calendar calls for them — live instantly, branded for your university."

05

Managing a community

The Community Profile is the hub for one group — branding, about text, member list, and feed. Coordinators monitor the student experience, post official content, and adjust settings where the role permits.

  1. Open a community from any list, carousel, or the Community tab.
  2. Read the About and settings; edit where staff editing is allowed.
  3. View the members list, search members, and remove a member if moderation tools exist.
  4. Scroll the feed, open posts, and Create post to pin welcome messages or announcements.
  5. Optionally Join the group to monitor it from a student's point of view.
Product

Coordinators see the same profile students do, plus staff powers — so they manage from inside the real experience rather than a separate admin console.

QA focus

Member list loads and searches; staff edit/remove appears only where permitted; coordinator posts publish to the feed; join state toggles correctly.

Marketing

"Run your communities from the inside — post announcements, welcome members, and keep groups thriving."

06

Moderation & reports

When configured, coordinators handle community-related reports — user flags about inappropriate posts or behaviour — alongside direct content moderation, working each case through a clear status workflow to resolution.

  1. Open a case from the Reports queue (when shown) or a moderation entry in the Community tools.
  2. Read the reporter, the reported content, the timestamp, and the community context.
  3. Take action on content — Remove or Hide a post/comment per policy.
  4. Move the report through Assigned → Under Review → Escalated → Resolved.
  5. Message the member via Chat to educate, or escalate severe cases to the University Super Admin.
Product

A shared four-state report lifecycle gives moderation a common language across roles, and the escalation path keeps the hardest cases moving up rather than stalling.

QA focus

Report status transitions persist; content remove/hide reflects for students; escalation routes to senior/USA review; resolving closes the case.

Marketing

"Keep conversations respectful — a clear moderation workflow means issues get handled, not ignored."

07

Chat & account

Direct messaging supports the governance work — especially explaining a rejection kindly — and the Profile tab holds account settings. Notifications surface new submissions, assigned reports, and milestones.

  1. Open the Chats tab to message creators and students about community decisions.
  2. After a rejection, message the creator to clarify naming or policy rules before they resubmit.
  3. Tap the bell for notifications — new submissions, assigned reports, membership milestones, chats.
  4. Use the envelope for deferred role invitations.
  5. Open Profile for account info, FAQs, terms, and logout; use Support to escalate beyond your authority.
Product

Pairing rejection with a chat path makes governance feel like coaching — creators get a person to talk to, not just a closed door.

QA focus

Threads load and send; notifications deep-link to the pending request, community, or report; the envelope shows pending invites; logout clears the session.

Marketing

"Decisions come with a conversation — coordinators guide students to get their community right."

Common questions

Community Coordinator FAQs

The questions coordinators, support, and demo audiences ask most often about the role.

What is the Community Coordinator responsible for?
Reviewing, publishing, and moderating communities within one university — clearing the pending queue and keeping student groups safe and on-policy.
How does community approval work?
New communities arrive in a pending queue. You review each one and approve it to publish or reject it with a reason. Students only ever see approved communities.
What happens when I reject a community?
It doesn’t go live, and the reason you give explains what needs to change. Rejected communities leave the active queue.
How do I handle reported posts or content?
Reported content surfaces in a moderation queue where you can review it and take action according to policy.
Can I create or approve events?
No — events belong to the Event Coordinator. You focus on communities; you can browse events like any member.
Can I invite or suspend users?
No. Inviting and suspending users is the University Super Admin’s job. Your tools are community-focused.
What’s the difference between me and the University Super Admin?
You own the community slice; the Super Admin owns the whole university — people, all queues, and governance — and can step into any area.
Do students see pending communities?
No. Only approved communities appear to students, which is exactly why the approval gate matters.
Am I limited to one university?
Yes — each role assignment is scoped to one university (and campus where configured).
What can students do in Community?
Submit a creation request (pending your approval), join approved communities, post in communities they have joined, see feeds with the latest post, like/comment/share, start chat, and view profiles. They never see unapproved communities in discovery.
What can Third-Party Coordinators do?
Same participation model as students — request creation (pending approval), join, post, engage — plus join suggestions for communities relevant to their interests or role.
Who gets notified about community activity?
Users are notified when a new community becomes available, when someone posts in a community they joined, and when their own creation request is approved or rejected (rejection includes the reason).
Can there be more than one Community Coordinator?
Yes. A university can run a team of any number of Community Coordinators sharing the pending queue, moderation, and official community creation.
